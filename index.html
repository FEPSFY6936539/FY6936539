An Introduction to Nucleosynthesis

The term nucleosynthesis is a concept that defines how the Universe evolved from a basic mixture of hydrogen and helium into a rich environment of elements that exist today. By tracing how different elements are produced in different stages of stellar evolution, scientists can build a clearer picture of how matter has changed over countless years and how these changes relate to the modern world. Nucleosynthesis, stellar or otherwise, began immediately after the Big Bang, when the Universe was extremely hot and dense. At that time, only the very lightest elements (majorly hydrogen, helium and a miniscule abundance of lithium) were formed. Heavier elements could not emerge because of the Universeâ€™s rapid expansion and cooling (Clayton, 2003). The emersion of these would require much more extreme and sustained conditions, such as those found inside stars. A major breakthrough in grasping this process came about in the mid-20th century from massively influential research that laid out a detailed description of how stars manufacture new elements throughout their lifetimes, forming the basis for the highly regarded modern theories of stellar evolution (Burbidge, Burbidge, Fowler and Hoyle, 1957).
